The Birth of the Mind:

How a Tiny Number of Genes Creates
The Complexities of Human Thought
Book by Gary Marcus, Site by Ram Zallan

Next

"The Mind is what the brain does."

It's common knowledge that drugs like anti-depressents target the brain to change peoples' moods, and that things like strokes that cause brain damage can change the way people act, and think, so it's clear that what people think of as their mind is really just what their brain does.

Just as the mind stems from the brain, the brain, like any other part of our bodies, stems from our genes. Though our brain, and therefore our mind, attitude, actions, and everything else we consider "ourselves," stems from our genes, that is not to say that everything about us is pre-decided and rigid.

Next

"The second biggest misconception people harbor about genetics: that it will be possible one day to determine... whether nature or nurture is 'more important'"

Without an environment, genes would be pointless in an animal, and without genes, the environment (along with essentially everything else) would be useless to the animal. Neither of the two supersedes the other, and yet neither would be of any use without the other. There has to be (and is) a balance of nature and nurture.

Next

"...we are, more than anything else, born to learn."

Though they might not seem like it, human babies are good at picking up patterns. Babies are born with a "skill" of imitation, something that not all animals are capable of. Babies were shown to mimic mouth movements (sticking out their tongue, pursing their lips, etc.) in studies; a possible explanation for this mimicry is humans' knack for picking up culture. This knack for picking up culture could very well stem from human's ability to communicate so well. Even babies who can't yet speak can tell the difference between language and gibberish. Some animals have certain calls or signals they communicate with, but nothing compares to the complexity and intricacy of human speech.

Next

"Nature provides a first draft, which experience then revises."

Though brains are hard-wired certain ways, change happens, through experience or otherwise, that can change the structure of the brain, and therefore, the mind. Infants have had to have a hemisphere of their brain removed (a very rare occurrence, only to stop monumental future ailments), yet the other hemisphere picked up some of the duties of the removed one, allowing the children to learn things like language almost as good, or just as good as children with both hemispheres.

Adult brains, in humans, aren't able to change as dramatically as those of children, but that's not to say some adult animals don't have that possibility. Studies have shown that parts of the brain devoted to use of a specific finger would transfer to the other fingers when the finger they were allocated for was amputated. Once there was no use for the area of the brain, it was gradually put back to work with the existing body parts.

Next

"Just because something is preprogrammed doesn’t mean it can’t also be reprogrammed."

Everyone knows that body parts can recover from injury. Even such injuries as breaking a bone heal after a while, so it shouldn't be surprising that the brain is the same way. The only bit about this that may be surprising is how the brain's cells are almost always the same, not being constantly replaced like other body parts. The organs, brain included, have redunencies in place so that if one part (for example, one hemisphere) fails, the other, or other similar structures, can take over its workload to an extent. If needed, cells in certain parts of the brain can change to take on new responsibilities for parts of the brain that may be damaged or missing.

Next

"Although there is unlikely to be any single gene for complex traits, there are likely to be many genes that profoundly influence those traits by tweaking... machinery that is already in place."

Genes don't seem to directly affect actions, in humans or other animals, but the parts of the brain that deals with said actions. No one gene causes people to do a specific complex action (like socializing), but there are most likely genes that control things like peoples' urge to have social comfort. People who are more likely to do certain actions than others don't do so because of a gene, but the combination of many genes, working together.

Next

"...the wiring between neurons is arguably the single thing that makes the brain special."

The wiring between neurons in the human brain allow us to take in data (vision, smell, etc.) process it, determine if any action should be taken, and deliver this decision (or lack thereof) to the various parts of your body. The speed of the human brain, thanks to the wiring, is what allows us to make snap reactions to things. Between the billions of neurons in the average human brain, there are trillions of connections intertwining them all.

After examining patients who had connections surgically severed to treat epilepsy, researchers saw how without the proper connections between the hemisphers of the brain, the patients could identity and name objects after seeing them on the right of their field of vision, but not the left. The lack of connection stopped their ability to transfer the information ("This is a spoon.") from one hemisphere to the other, causing them to not recognize the object.

Next

"...experience itself can modify the expression of genes."

"Not every gene, nor every brain connection can be modified by experience. Each species has a different way of connecting experience to gene expression, and these different links, make possible different kinds of learning."

Things as simple as living condition in rodents can affect a multitude of genes, controlling a number of things from DNA reproduction, to an increased number of synapses per neuron. A driving factor that keeps living things living is their ability to change their nervous system based on external stimuli.

Next

"...the ability to learn starts with the ability to remember."

For an organism to learn, it needs to be able to store the memory of what it has to learn from. No one is quite sure on how exactly the brain stores and retrieves memories. There are areas that the brain uses to "store" memories throughout the brain, each with a different use. The hippocampus is used more for spatial (or location-based) memories, the amygdala for emotion-based memories, and so on. Mice with genes controlling parts of their hippocampus had no trouble connecting a sound to an electric shock, but couldn't get through a maze, while those with genes that affected their amygdala changed could get through the maze fine, but didn't pick up on the relationship between the sound and the shock.

Next

"All evolution arises in one way or another from some kind of change in genetic code."

"...an A changed to a C, a T to a G... mutations can lead to disorders, but they can also lead to useful evolutionary innovation."

Though mutations can occasionally be good, spreading new traits through future generations, they can be just as bad. Simple errors in nucleotides in DNA (an AGC turning into a CGA, for example), can cause major problems in the long run. Simple things like a single "misplaced" T could cause a different amino acid to be formed by the nucleotide, which can, in the long run, be catastrophic.

One result of mutation could be duplication of nucleotides. Not every nucleotide turns into the protein or amino acid it was meant to, and a second copy of the nucleotide could either aid, or hinder the organism. Conditions like Down Syndrome are thought to be caused by duplication of genes, and, though there can be pluses to having multiple of the same gene, there are also many negatives.

Next

"A major part of what the brain does is to communicate signals from one place to another."

"It takes information from the senses, analyses that information, and translates it into commands that get sent back to the muscles."

Single-celled organisms may not need fast or accurate communications between various parts of their "bodies," but humans, with billions of cells, rely on both accuracy and speed of communications in their body for every-day life. The nervous system works great for this, with neurons "talking" to each other through electrical impulses.

Next

"...we cannot simply point to a particular spot in the brain and say this is the language area, this is the neural circuit that makes this brain a uniquely human brain."

The human brain, and all other brains for that matter, are products of evolution. None of the changes leading up to our "current model" happened overnight, but over the course of generations, and generations, and generations, through species, and species, and species. Though thousands of genes help in the making of arms and legs, only a select few are specific to either of the limbs. No one part of the brain, nor one gene, controls one specific action of the human body, yet all our genes contribute to create us.
